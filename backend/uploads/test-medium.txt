Introduction to Renewable Energy Systems

Renewable energy refers to power generated from natural sources that replenish faster
than they are consumed, such as sunlight, wind, rain, tides, and geothermal heat. Unlike
fossil fuels, these sources do not run out on a human timescale and typically produce far
fewer greenhouse gas emissions during operation.

Solar Power
Solar power is captured using photovoltaic (PV) panels or concentrated solar power (CSP)
systems. PV panels convert sunlight directly into electricity using semiconductor materials,
most commonly silicon. When photons strike the panel, they knock electrons loose, creating
a flow of direct current (DC) electricity, which is then converted to alternating current
(AC) using an inverter. Solar panel efficiency typically ranges between 15% and 22% for
commercial panels, though laboratory prototypes have achieved higher rates. Solar farms can
be installed on rooftops for residential use or across large open fields for utility-scale
generation.

Wind Power
Wind turbines convert kinetic energy from moving air into electricity using large rotating
blades connected to a generator. Modern wind turbines are typically 80 to 120 meters tall,
with blades spanning 40 to 60 meters. Wind farms are often built offshore, where wind speeds
are more consistent and stronger than on land. A single large offshore turbine can generate
enough electricity to power thousands of homes. One challenge with wind power is
intermittency, since electricity generation depends heavily on wind speed and availability.

Hydropower
Hydropower generates electricity by using the force of moving water, typically from dams,
to spin turbines connected to generators. It is one of the oldest and most widely used forms
of renewable energy, providing a significant share of global electricity. Pumped-storage
hydropower plants can also act as large-scale energy storage systems, pumping water uphill
during periods of low electricity demand and releasing it to generate power during peak
demand.

Geothermal Energy
Geothermal energy harnesses heat from within the Earth, typically accessed through wells
drilled into underground reservoirs of steam or hot water. This heat can be used directly
for heating buildings or converted into electricity using steam turbines. Geothermal power
plants are most commonly built in geologically active regions, such as areas near tectonic
plate boundaries, where underground heat is closer to the surface.

Energy Storage
Because solar and wind power are intermittent, energy storage systems play a critical role
in making renewable energy reliable. Lithium-ion batteries are currently the most common
storage technology, used both in home battery systems and large grid-scale installations.
Other storage methods include pumped hydro storage, compressed air energy storage, and
emerging technologies like flow batteries and hydrogen storage.

Challenges and Future Outlook
Despite rapid growth, renewable energy adoption faces challenges including high upfront
installation costs, the need for improved energy storage, and updates to aging electrical
grid infrastructure to handle variable power inputs. However, costs for solar panels and
wind turbines have fallen dramatically over the past decade, making renewables increasingly
cost-competitive with fossil fuels in many regions. Many countries have set ambitious targets
to increase the share of renewables in their electricity mix over the coming decades.
